\section{Introduction and Objectives}

This report applies scanned-wavelength absorption spectroscopy to recover temperature, pressure, and CO mole fraction from a set of CO absorption features near \SI{2008.5}{\per\centi\meter} in a shock-tube experiment.\footnote{Public code archive: \url{https://github.com/rverleur/mock_lab}.} The dataset includes a baseline scan that defines the unabsorbed transmission, an etalon scan that provides the frequency calibration, and repeated shock-tube scans containing the absorption spectra of interest. The reduction converts detector voltage to absorbance for each sweep, fits the resulting spectrum with a constrained three-line Voigt model, and reconstructs the gas state as a function of time with propagated uncertainty.

\section{Theory}

\subsection*{Shock-Tube Context}

In a shock tube, rupture of the diaphragm drives an incident shock into the driven gas and produces the compressed state behind it, usually called Region~2. Reflection of that shock from the end wall creates the hotter, denser Region~5 state. For shock-tube absorption measurements, Region~5 is usually the state of interest because it is closer to the intended thermodynamic condition and remains nearly constant over the measurement interval. The optical section is therefore placed near the end wall so that the delay between incident- and reflected-shock arrival is short while the flow remains sufficiently uniform for spectroscopy.

Shock tubes are useful for chemical-kinetics measurements because they create a well-defined thermodynamic jump and therefore a well-defined time zero for high-temperature chemistry to begin. In the present analysis, the processed absorption data distinguish the early Region~2 portion of the record from the later Region~5 portion, and the transition between those states is visible in the recovered histories shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:state-history}.

\subsection*{Absorbance and Beer--Lambert Law}
The starting point is the one-dimensional radiative-transfer equation. For a uniform, non-scattering medium,

\begin{equation}
  \frac{dI_{\tilde{\nu}}}{dx}
  =
  k_{\tilde{\nu}}\left(I_{\tilde{\nu}}^{\mathrm{bb}}-I_{\tilde{\nu}}\right)
\end{equation}

where $I_{\tilde{\nu}}$ is the spectral intensity, $I_{\tilde{\nu}}^{\mathrm{bb}}$ is the blackbody source term, and $k_{\tilde{\nu}}$ is the spectral absorption coefficient. For the present measurements, the laser intensity is much larger than gas self-emission, so the emission term is neglected and the transmitted intensity is written as

\begin{equation}
  I_{\tilde{\nu}}(L)
  =
  I_{\tilde{\nu},0}\exp(-\kappa_{\tilde{\nu}}L)
\end{equation}

Under the independent-line approximation, the spectral absorption coefficient is

\begin{equation}
  \kappa_{\tilde{\nu}}
  =
  \sum_i \kappa_{\tilde{\nu},i}
  =
  PX\sum_i S_i(T)\phi_i(\tilde{\nu})
\end{equation}

where $P$ is pressure in atm, $X$ is absorber mole fraction, $S_i(T)$ is the line strength, and $\phi_i(\tilde{\nu})$ is a normalized line-shape function. In the present reduction, the tabulated HiTEMP line strengths are converted immediately to pressure-based units of cm$^{-2}$/atm, so the pressure-form Beer--Lambert expression above can be used directly. The measured quantity is absorbance,

\begin{equation}
  \alpha(\tilde{\nu})
  =
  \kappa_{\tilde{\nu}}L
  =
  PXL\sum_i S_i(T)\phi_i(\tilde{\nu})
\end{equation}

For a normalized line shape, the integrated area of line $i$ is simply $PXL\,S_i(T)$. This relation is used later when the fitted area of the strongest transition is converted into CO mole fraction.

\subsection*{Temperature-Dependent Line Strength}

Reference line strengths from HiTEMP are scaled from $T_0$ to the local gas temperature with the following correction \cite{rothman2010hitemp,hitempco2019},

\begin{equation}
  S_i(T)=S_i(T_0)\frac{Q(T_0)}{Q(T)}\frac{T_0}{T}
  \exp\!\left[-\frac{hcE_i''}{k_B}\!\left(\frac{1}{T}-\frac{1}{T_0}\right)\right]
  \frac{1-\exp\!\left(-\frac{hc\tilde{\nu}_{0,i}}{k_B T}\right)}{1-\exp\!\left(-\frac{hc\tilde{\nu}_{0,i}}{k_B T_0}\right)}
\end{equation}

Here $Q(T)$ is the partition sum, $E_i''$ is the lower-state energy, and $\tilde{\nu}_{0,i}$ is the line center. The line strengths and partition-sum values used in this reduction were both taken from the HiTEMP dataset after conversion to the working units noted above. The correction accounts for the changing partition sum, the Boltzmann population of the lower state, and stimulated emission. Because the three scanned lines have very different lower-state energies and reference strengths, their relative absorbance provides useful temperature sensitivity.

\subsection*{Line Shapes and Collisional Broadening}

For the present spectra, the relevant broadening mechanisms are Doppler broadening from thermal motion and collisional broadening from molecular collisions; natural broadening is negligible. Their combined effect is represented with a Voigt profile,

\begin{equation}
  \phi_i(\tilde{\nu})
  =
  \phi_{V,i}\!\left[\tilde{\nu}-\tilde{\nu}_{0,i};\,\Delta\tilde{\nu}_{D,i},\,\Delta\tilde{\nu}_{C,i}\right]
\end{equation}

where $\Delta\tilde{\nu}_{D,i}$ and $\Delta\tilde{\nu}_{C,i}$ are the Doppler and collisional full widths at half maximum. The Gaussian contribution controls the thermal line core, while the Lorentzian contribution controls the pressure-broadened wings. The Doppler width is

\begin{equation}
  \Delta\tilde{\nu}_{D,i}
  =
  2\tilde{\nu}_{0,i}
  \sqrt{\frac{2k_B T \ln 2}{mc^2}}
\end{equation}

If the tabulated broadening coefficients are written as Lorentz half-widths $\gamma_{k,i,\mathrm{ref}}$, the collisional FWHM is

\begin{equation}
  \Delta\tilde{\nu}_{C,i}(T)
  =
  2P\sum_k X_k\,\gamma_{k,i,\mathrm{ref}}
  \left(\frac{T_{\mathrm{ref}}}{T}\right)^{n_{k,i}}
\end{equation}

where $X_k$ is the mole fraction of collision partner $k$ and $n_{k,i}$ is the temperature exponent. In this problem, the Doppler contribution carries most of the direct temperature sensitivity, while the collisional contribution carries most of the direct pressure sensitivity. The fit therefore uses the Doppler width to infer temperature, and the fitted Lorentz width of the strongest line helps recover pressure.

\section{Experimental Setup and Procedure}

Measurements were acquired in Purdue's High-Pressure Shock Tube with a driven gas of 2\% CO, 1.8\% \ce{CO2}, and balance \ce{N2}. The target state for Region~5 was approximately \SI{4000}{K} and \SI{1}{atm}, and the shock-tube diameter gave a \SI{10.32}{cm} absorption path length.

A QCL near \SI{4.98}{\micro\meter} was used to scan the CO feature group by modulating the drive current at \SI{300}{kHz}. Because QCL tuning is partly thermal, the exact frequency trajectory varies slightly from sweep to sweep even under nominally identical drive conditions. An independent etalon measurement was therefore used to map sweep time to relative wavenumber.

The reduction uses three datasets. The baseline scan, acquired with no absorber in the beam path, defines the unabsorbed transmission used in the absorbance calculation. The etalon scan uses a 3 in. solid germanium Fabry--Perot interferometer whose fringes provide the relative time-to-wavenumber calibration. The spacing between adjacent transmission maxima is the free spectral range,

\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FSR}=\frac{1}{2nL}\approx\SI{0.0163}{\per\centi\meter},
\end{equation}

for $L=\SI{7.62}{cm}$ and $n=4.019$. The shock-tube measurement supplies the transmitted-intensity sweeps from which the absorbance spectra and state histories are recovered. Each file also contains a TTL reference used to register the repeated sweeps on a common local time axis.

\section{Data Reduction}

Each MAT file contains a detector channel and a TTL-like reference channel. The reference is thresholded to identify sweep boundaries, and a fixed phase offset of \SI{2.2}{\micro\second} is applied before reshaping. This discards the first partial sweep and ensures that each retained sweep contains a consistent pre-ramp baseline, the useful laser ramp, and a short trailing baseline.

The registered baseline sweeps are averaged to form a single reference sweep. A linear baseline is then fit through the flat edges of the average baseline and each shock sweep and subtracted before absorbance is formed. With detector voltage assumed linear in optical intensity, absorbance is computed as $A=-\ln(I/I_0)$, where $I_0$ is the corrected baseline and $I$ is the corrected shocked-gas signal. Each shock sweep is also peak-scaled to the corrected average baseline so that slow amplitude drift does not appear as false absorbance.

The retained analysis window is restricted to the active \SIrange{1.0}{2.75}{V} portion of the ramp. The lower cutoff excludes the low-signal beginning of the sweep, where the transmission ratio is noisy, and the upper cutoff avoids the high-voltage end near the scan maximum.

A representative time-domain reduction is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:shock-time}. The left panel compares the corrected average baseline with one corrected shock sweep. Outside the absorbing portions of the scan, the two traces nearly coincide, while the departures between them mark the CO absorption features. The right panel shows the corresponding absorbance in the retained part of the sweep before conversion to frequency.

\subsection*{Etalon Calibration}

The etalon data convert sweep time to relative wavenumber. Each etalon sweep is edge-corrected, fringe peaks are detected on the registered scans, the peak times are averaged by fringe order, and those times are assigned equally spaced relative wavenumbers using the known free spectral range. A quartic polynomial is then fit to the averaged peak locations to capture the modest nonlinearity of the QCL sweep. This is a relative rather than absolute calibration: it gives a consistent wavenumber axis within the scan window but does not lock the spectrum to an external frequency standard.

The etalon calibration is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:etalon-cal}. The left panel shows a representative corrected etalon sweep with the detected fringe peaks. The right panel shows the quartic time-to-wavenumber fit and its residuals. The residuals remain small compared with the etalon free spectral range, so the quartic is adequate over the useful scan interval.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection*{Voigt Fit and Data Extraction}

Each usable absorbance spectrum is fit with a constrained sum of three Voigt profiles using nonlinear least squares. The optimizer works on a reduced eight-parameter description. Those parameters are the shared temperature, the anchor center of P(0,31), a small center correction for P(2,20), one collisional half-width for P(0,31), one shared collisional half-width for P(2,20) and P(3,14), the integrated area of P(0,31), and a linear baseline offset and slope. The per-line centers, widths, and areas are then reconstructed from this reduced parameterization.

The fit constraints were implemented directly. The weakest transition, P(3,14), is held at a fixed offset from P(0,31) and forced to share the P(2,20) collisional width. Only the strongest integrated area is fit freely; the weaker areas follow the temperature-dependent line-strength ratios. A small linear baseline is retained inside the spectral fit to absorb residual broadband mismatch, and the previous successful fit seeds the next sweep. Together, these choices reduce line swapping and unstable tradeoff in fitted areas between overlapping features.

The fit is then used to extract the quantities needed for the state solve. Temperature was fit directly. Pressure and CO mole fraction are solved jointly from the P(0,31) linewidth and integrated area with temperature held fixed at its fitted value. For this step, the broadening coefficients were taken from Mathews et al.\ \cite{mathews2021highbandwidth}. The CO--CO$_2$ broadening coefficients were assumed equal to the CO--N$_2$ values, and the pressure inferred from the collisional width used the adopted 0.84 scaling factor recommended for this dataset. The weaker transitions therefore matter mainly because they stabilize the spectral decomposition and improve the temperature estimate.

A representative fit is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:voigt-fit}. The measured absorbance and total model agree closely, and the residual remains small relative to the peak absorbance. The dominant peak is anchored by P(0,31), the lower-wavenumber feature is dominated by P(2,20), and P(3,14) appears as a weak high-wavenumber shoulder.

\subsection*{Recovered State Histories}

The recovered temperature, pressure, and CO mole-fraction histories are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:state-history}. The first few points, up to about \SI{50}{\micro\second}, correspond to Region~2 behind the incident shock but before arrival of the reflected shock. That interval is short, but the recovered CO mole fraction near \num{0.02} is consistent with the initial driven-gas composition.

At about \SI{50}{\micro\second}, the reflected shock reaches the test section and the recovered state moves into Region~5. The pressure jumps to nearly \SI{1}{atm}, and the temperature rises at the same time to about \SI{4000}{K}, matching the target condition. The CO mole fraction increases more gradually and then levels off just below 4\%, which is consistent with substantial and potentially near-complete dissociation of the initial \ce{CO2} fraction under the reflected-shock conditions.

That Region~5 state remains approximately steady for about \SI{350}{\micro\second}. After that, pressure rises while temperature falls, and the recovered CO mole fraction also decreases. This late-time behavior is consistent with both recombination toward \ce{CO2} and dilution by driver gas, but it should be interpreted more cautiously than the initial reflected-shock plateau.

The uncertainty bands in Fig.~\ref{fig:state-history} remain relatively narrow through much of the early and mid-time record, then widen toward the end as the spectra weaken and the fitted pressure becomes more sensitive to linewidth uncertainty. A full description of their construction is given in the next section.

\section{Uncertainty}

\subsection*{Fit and Spectroscopic Contributions}

Two independent uncertainty sources were carried through the analysis. The first is local fit uncertainty from the constrained nonlinear least-squares problem. It is estimated from the fit Jacobian, which gives a local covariance for the reduced fit parameters under the usual linearization near the optimum. That covariance is then propagated through the parameter expansion and state-extraction steps to obtain uncertainty in temperature, pressure, and CO mole fraction.

The second source is uncertainty in the spectroscopic inputs. It was treated with a full Monte Carlo refit using jittered spectroscopic parameters. For each of 1000 trials, the sampled parameters were drawn uniformly within the bounds implied by the HiTEMP/HITRAN uncertainty data, consistent with NIST Technical Note 1297 \cite{taylor1994uncertainty}, and the full constrained Voigt-fit and state-solve workflow was repeated.

The two sources were treated as linearly independent and combined by root-sum-square of their 95\% half-widths. The resulting total uncertainty is included in Fig.~\ref{fig:state-history}. In Region~5 the combined uncertainty is dominated by the spectroscopic contribution, while at late time the weakening spectra make the fit term larger.

\section{Conclusions}

The provided scans were reduced to baseline-corrected absorbance spectra, calibrated on a relative frequency axis with the etalon, fit with a constrained three-line Voigt model, and converted into time-resolved estimates of temperature, pressure, and CO mole fraction. The recovered histories identify the expected sequence of states: an early Region~2 interval, followed near \SI{50}{\micro\second} by a Region~5 state close to the target condition of \SI{\sim 4000}{K} and \SI{\sim 1}{atm}. The CO mole fraction rises more gradually than the thermodynamic variables and then levels off just below 4\%, consistent with substantial dissociation of the initial \ce{CO2} fraction.

The main limitations are the modeling assumptions required to make the reduction stable from the absorption data alone. Pressure extraction treats all non-CO collisions as a single nitrogen-equivalent bath gas and applies the adopted 0.84 scaling factor, neither of which yields a fully species-resolved reacting-mixture broadening model. The calibration is relative rather than absolute, and the spectral fit constrains the weakest transition by fixing its offset from the anchor line and forcing it to share a collisional width with the secondary line. Those choices improve stability but likely reduce absolute accuracy when the real line positions or broadening behavior depart from the model.

If the measurement were repeated, the clearest improvements would be an absolute frequency reference and a better broadening model for the reacting mixture. Additional benefit would likely come from stronger independent constraints on the weak transitions, either through improved signal-to-noise ratio or a scan window with better line separation. A public repository for the reduction code and plotting scripts is available at \url{https://github.com/rverleur/mock_lab}.
